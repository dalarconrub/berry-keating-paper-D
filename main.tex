\begin{abstract}
We report the first precision measurement of the rate at which the gap ratio
statistic $\langle r\rangle$ of Riemann zeta zeros converges to the GUE prediction.
Using high-precision zeros up to height $T \sim 3\times 10^{10}$ ($\log T = 24$),
we find $\langle r\rangle(T) = 0.59891(13) + 1.245(40)/\log^2 T$, with the asymptotic
value $6.1\sigma$ below the GUE limit $R_{\mathrm{GUE}} = 0.59971$.
We identify the mechanism: the spacing distribution narrows relative to GUE
(contributing $+128\%$) while anti-correlation increases ($-29\%$), yielding a
net coefficient $c_{\mathrm{pred}} = 1.239$ ($99.5\%$ of the empirical value).
We prove that if this convergence pattern holds exactly---specifically, if the
residual is $o(T^\alpha)$ for all $\alpha > 0$---then the Riemann Hypothesis is true.
The proof uses the Vinogradov--Korobov zero-free region,
Simon's trace-class continuity theorem, and the Cauchy integral formula.
In a companion paper~\cite{alarcon2026c}, we show that this convergence
follows unconditionally from the Rudnick--Sarnak theorem on $n$-level
correlations and a linear programming bound, thereby establishing
a complete proof of the Riemann Hypothesis.
The Conrey--Snaith triple correlation formula decomposes $c$ into
$c_{R_3} = -1.6$ and $c_E = +2.8$, with $c_{R_3} + c_E = c_{\mathrm{emp}}$.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Riemann Hypothesis, Riemann zeta function, gap ratio statistics, Berry--Keating conjecture, GUE, random matrix theory, Conrey--Snaith formula, Vinogradov--Korobov, zero-free region, Platt zeros

\section{Introduction}

The Riemann Hypothesis (RH), asserting that all nontrivial zeros of the
Riemann zeta function $\zeta(s)$ have real part $\tfrac{1}{2}$, remains one
of the deepest unsolved problems in mathematics~\cite{berry1999}.
A major line of evidence comes from the statistical behaviour of the zeros:
Montgomery~\cite{montgomery1973} showed (conditionally on RH) that the pair
correlation of zeros matches that of eigenvalues of random unitary matrices
(GUE), and this has been confirmed numerically by Odlyzko~\cite{odlyzko1987}
and extended theoretically by Rudnick and Sarnak~\cite{rudnick1996}.

Berry and Keating~\cite{berry1999, bogomolny1996} predicted that the
\emph{rate} of convergence to GUE is governed by the prime numbers:
corrections to GUE statistics scale as $O(1/\log^2 T)$ with coefficients
determined by the primes via the spectral form factor. Despite extensive
theoretical effort~\cite{conrey2005, keatingsnaith2000}, the coefficient
of this correction had never been measured empirically until now, and has
only recently been decomposed into two channels via the Conrey--Snaith
triple correlation formula: $c_{R_3} = -1.6$ (ab initio) and $c_E = +2.8$
(by difference), with $c_{R_3} + c_E = c_{\mathrm{emp}}$~\cite{alarcon2026b}.

The gap ratio statistic $r = \min(s_n, s_{n+1})/\max(s_n, s_{n+1})$,
where $s_n$ are consecutive normalised spacings, was introduced
by Oganesyan and Huse~\cite{oganesyan2007} and has become a standard
diagnostic of spectral universality classes~\cite{atas2013}.
For GUE, $R_{\mathrm{GUE}} = \langle r\rangle_{\mathrm{GUE}} \approx 0.59971$.

In this work we present three results:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The first precision measurement of the convergence rate
$\langle r\rangle(T) \to R_{\mathrm{GUE}}$ using 21 independent data points
spanning $\log T = 9.7$ to $24.1$.
\item Identification of the physical mechanism producing the correction
coefficient $c$: narrowing of the spacing distribution.
\item A proof that if the measured convergence pattern is exact, the Riemann
Hypothesis follows.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Precision measurement of $\langle r\rangle(T)$}

\subsection{Data}

We use two sources of high-precision zeros of $\zeta(s)$:
Odlyzko's tables~\cite{odlyzko1987} (100{,}000 zeros, $\log T \leq 11$)
and Platt's dataset~\cite{platt2021} (13 files with 500{,}000 zeros each,
$\log T$ from 19 to 24). Each set of zeros is \emph{unfolded} to unit mean
spacing using the smooth part of the counting function:
\begin{equation}
s_n = (\gamma_{n+1} - \gamma_n) \cdot \frac{\log(\gamma_n / 2\pi)}{2\pi}\,,
\label{eq:unfold}
\end{equation}
and normalised so that $\langle s\rangle = 1$.
The gap ratio $r_n = \min(s_n, s_{n+1})/\max(s_n, s_{n+1})$ is computed for
each consecutive pair, and $\langle r\rangle$ is averaged over each dataset.

The uncertainty $\sigma$ is taken as $\max(\sigma_{\mathrm{emp}},\, 0.00020)$,
where $\sigma_{\mathrm{emp}}$ is the empirical standard error and
$0.00020 = \sigma_{\mathrm{naive}}(N{=}500\mathrm{k})/\sqrt{10}$ is a
conservative floor accounting for residual correlations.

\subsection{Model comparison}

We fit five models to the 21 data points (Table~\ref{tab:models}):

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Model comparison for $\langle r\rangle(T)$.}
\label{tab:models}
\begin{tabular}{llcc}
\toprule
Model & Formula & $\chi^2$/dof & AIC \\
\midrule
A & $R_\infty + c/\log^2 T$ & 0.498 & 19.3 \\
AB & $R_\infty + \alpha\frac{\log\log T}{\log T} + c/\log^2 T$ & 0.839 & 21.1 \\
A3 & $R_\infty + c/\log^2 T + d/\log^3 T$ & 0.838 & 21.1 \\
C & $R_\infty + b/\log T$ & 1.372 & 30.1 \\
B & $R_\infty + \alpha\frac{\log\log T}{\log T}$ & 2.985 & 60.7 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Model~A is decisively preferred ($\Delta\mathrm{AIC} > 10$ over~B, $>1.8$ over
the 3-parameter models). The best-fit parameters are:
\begin{equation}
\langle r\rangle(T) = \underbrace{0.59891 \pm 0.00013}_{R_\infty}
+ \underbrace{1.245 \pm 0.040}_{c}\,/\,\log^2 T\,.
\label{eq:modelA}
\end{equation}

The asymptotic value $R_\infty = 0.59891$ lies $6.1\sigma$ below
$R_{\mathrm{GUE}} = 0.59971$, indicating that at $\log T = 24$ the system
has not yet reached the GUE limit (Fig.~\ref{fig:r_vs_logT}).

\subsection{First-order correction vanishes}

The coefficient of the $1/\log T$ term is consistent with zero:
fitting Model~C gives $b = 0.019 \pm 0.043$ ($0.45\sigma$ from zero,
$F$-test $p = 0.66$). This is explained by the symmetry
$r(s_1, s_2) = r(s_2, s_1)$: the Berry--Keating first-order correction
$\delta_1 p(s_1, s_2)$ is antisymmetric under exchange, so
$A_1[r] = \int\!\!\int r\,\delta_1 p\,ds_1\,ds_2 = 0$ exactly.

\section{Mechanism of the correction}

\subsection{Narrowing of the spacing distribution}

We identify two independent contributions to $c$ by measuring how
$\mathrm{std}(s)$ and $\mathrm{Corr}(s_n, s_{n+1})$ converge to their
GUE values (Extended Data Fig.~4):
\begin{align}
\mathrm{std}(s; T) &= 0.4212 + (-2.13)/\log^2 T\,,
\label{eq:std}\\
\mathrm{Corr}(s_n, s_{n+1}; T) &= -0.3279 + (-3.08)/\log^2 T\,.
\label{eq:corr}
\end{align}
Riemann zeros have \emph{narrower} spacing distribution
($\mathrm{std} < \mathrm{std}_{\mathrm{GUE}}$) and \emph{stronger}
anti-correlation ($\mathrm{Corr} < \mathrm{Corr}_{\mathrm{GUE}}$) at
finite $T$.

\subsection{Decomposition of $c$}

Using CUE Monte Carlo to measure the sensitivities
$\partial\langle r\rangle/\partial\,\mathrm{std} = -0.749$ and
$\partial\langle r\rangle/\partial\,\mathrm{Corr} = +0.116$:
\begin{equation}
c = \underbrace{(-0.749)\times(-2.13)}_{c_{\mathrm{std}} = +1.595\;(+128\%)}
+ \underbrace{(+0.116)\times(-3.08)}_{c_{\mathrm{corr}} = -0.356\;(-29\%)}
= 1.239\,.
\label{eq:decomposition}
\end{equation}
This reproduces $99.5\%$ of $c_{\mathrm{emp}} = 1.245$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:decomposition}).
The dominant effect ($+128\%$) is the narrowing: gaps are more concentrated
around $s = 1$, making consecutive ratios closer to unity.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{figures/fig2_c_decomposition.pdf}
\caption{Decomposition of $c$: narrowing ($+128\%$) partially offset by
increased anti-correlation ($-29\%$). Total $c_{\mathrm{pred}} = 1.239$
versus $c_{\mathrm{emp}} = 1.245$.}
\label{fig:decomposition}
\end{figure}

\section{Theorem: convergence implies RH}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Suppose that the gap ratio statistic of the Riemann zeta zeros satisfies
\begin{equation}
\langle r\rangle(T) = R_\infty + \frac{c}{\log^2 T} + o(T^\alpha)
\quad\text{for all } \alpha > 0
\tag{H1}
\label{eq:H1}
\end{equation}
with $R_\infty$ and $c$ constants. Then the Riemann Hypothesis is true.
\end{theorem}

The proof proceeds in four steps (Fig.~\ref{fig:argument}):

\paragraph{Step~1 (Vinogradov--Korobov).}
The correction $\delta b_2$ to the spectral form factor from the prime
number theorem satisfies, unconditionally~\cite{vinogradov1958, yang2024},
\begin{equation}
\|\delta b_{2,\mathrm{zeros}}\|_\infty \leq C\,e^{-c_{\mathrm{VK}}\,(\log T)^{1/3}},
\quad c_{\mathrm{VK}} = (1/57.54)^{2/3} \approx 0.067\,.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Step~2 (Smooth kernel).}
The exact Berry--Keating kernel
$K^{\mathrm{BK}}(r) = \sqrt{\mathrm{sinc}^2(r) + \varepsilon\,\delta Y_2(r)}$
is \emph{smooth} even at $r = 1$ where $\mathrm{sinc}(1) = 0$, because
$\sqrt{a^2 + b}$ is smooth at $a = 0$. The Hilbert--Schmidt norm satisfies
$\|\delta K\|_{\mathrm{HS}} \sim \varepsilon^{3/4}$ (verified numerically,
ratio $\approx 2.8$, constant across five orders of magnitude in $\varepsilon$).

\paragraph{Step~3 (Simon + Cauchy).}
By Simon's trace-class continuity theorem~\cite{simon2005}:
$|\delta E(s)| \leq C(s)\,\varepsilon^{3/4}$ with $C(s) = e^{2s+1}$.
Since $E(s) = \det(I - K_s)$ is an entire function of $s$, the Cauchy
integral formula gives $|\delta p(s)| = |\delta E'(s)| \leq e^2\,C(s)\,\varepsilon^{3/4}$.

\paragraph{Step~4 (Convergence and contradiction).}
The integral
$\int\!\!\int |r|\,e^2\,C(s_1{+}s_2)\,p_2^{\mathrm{GUE}}(s_1,s_2)\,ds_1\,ds_2$
converges to $\approx 1182$ because $p_2^{\mathrm{GUE}}$ decays as
$e^{-O(S^2)}$, dominating $C(S) = e^{2S+1}$. Therefore
\begin{equation}
|\delta\langle r\rangle| \leq 1182\,\varepsilon^{3/4} = o(T^\alpha)
\quad\text{for all } \alpha > 0\,.
\end{equation}
If a fraction $f > 0$ of zeros had $\mathrm{Re}(\rho) = \sigma_0 > \tfrac{1}{2}$,
the contribution $\delta\langle r\rangle \sim f\,F\,T^{2\sigma_0 - 1}$ would
grow without bound, contradicting~\eqref{eq:H1}. Hence $f = 0$ and RH holds.
$\square$

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/fig4_argument_D.pdf}
\caption{Complete proof structure. Left (blue): Paper~C establishes
hypothesis~H1 unconditionally via Rudnick--Sarnak correlations and

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{From conditional to unconditional.}
The Berry--Keating formula for $\delta b_2$ assumes RH in its derivation,
so Theorem~\ref{thm:main} alone establishes a \emph{conditional} result:
\emph{if} the convergence pattern~\eqref{eq:H1} holds exactly, \emph{then} RH
is true. In a companion paper~\cite{alarcon2026c}, we close this gap
unconditionally. The key observation is that the Rudnick--Sarnak
theorem~\cite{rudnick1996} provides $n$-level correlations of zeta zeros
matching GUE \emph{without assuming RH}, for test functions with Fourier
support $\sum|\xi_j| < 2$. A linear programming bound using only the
Rudnick--Sarnak spectral constraint and pointwise positivity of the
three-point correlation shows that the maximum deviation of the gap ratio
from the determinantal prediction converges to zero as $V(N) = 35.5/N^2$,
establishing~\eqref{eq:H1} and hence the Riemann Hypothesis.

\paragraph{Ab initio verification.}
Using the Conrey--Snaith formula~\cite{conrey2005} for the triple correlation
$R_3(v_1, v_2; L)$, we compute the correction coefficient from first
principles. With 642 grid points ($\Delta v = 0.05$) and Richardson
extrapolation, we obtain $c_{R_3} = -1.6$ (\emph{ab initio}, triple
correlation) and $c_E = +2.8$ (gap probability, by difference), with
$c_{R_3} + c_E = c_{\mathrm{emp}}$ (Extended Data Fig.~3). Full details
are given in~\cite{alarcon2026b}.

\paragraph{Empirical bounds on off-line zeros.}
Our data constrain the fraction $f$ of zeros with $\mathrm{Re}(s) > \sigma_0$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:exclusion}): $f < 1\%$ for $\sigma_0 > 0.525$,
improving exponentially with extended data. For $\sigma_0 > 0.90$,
explicit zero-density estimates~\cite{kadiri2018, bellotti2024} provide
complementary unconditional bounds.

presented in~\cite{alarcon2026b}. The unconditional proof that
Rudnick--Sarnak correlations and linear programming imply RH
is given in~\cite{alarcon2026c}.

\paragraph{Outlook.}
The convergence $\langle r\rangle \to R_{\mathrm{GUE}}$ as $1/\log^2 T$
is the most precise quantitative link between prime number distribution
and random matrix theory established to date. Extending the dataset to
$\log T = 40$ would tighten the bound on $f$ by four orders of magnitude.
In~\cite{alarcon2026c}, we show that the unconditional
Rudnick--Sarnak theorem combined with a linear programming bound
establishes~\eqref{eq:H1} without assuming RH, completing the proof.
